The Mughal Empire (1526-1857)

The Mughal Empire was an early modern empire that controlled much of South Asia between the 16th and 19th centuries. For some two centuries, it stretched from the outer fringes of the Indus basin in the west, northern Afghanistan in the northwest, and Kashmir in the north, to the highlands of present-day Assam and Bangladesh in the east, and the uplands of the Deccan Plateau in South India.

Key Historical Phases

  • Foundation (1526): Founded by Babur, a Central Asian ruler and descendant of Timur and Genghis Khan, after his victory at the First Battle of Panipat.
  • Expansion & Zenith (1556-1707): Under emperors like Akbar the Great, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb, the empire became a global power, commanding nearly 24% of the world's GDP.
  • Decline (1707-1857): After Aurangzeb's death, the empire weakened due to internal strife, external invasions, and the rise of regional powers. The British East India Company eventually took control after the Indian Rebellion of 1857.

    • Cultural & Architectural Legacy

    The Mughals are world-renowned for their unique Indo-Islamic architectural style, characterized by:
  • The Taj Mahal: Built by Shah Jahan in memory of his wife, Mumtaz Mahal.
  • The Red Fort: A massive sandstone fortification in Delhi.
  • Administrative Innovation: The Mansabdari system, which organized the military and bureaucracy into a single centralized hierarchy.
